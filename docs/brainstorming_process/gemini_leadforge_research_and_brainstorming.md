# **Foundations of Dynamic Structural Causal Models for Synthetic Customer Journey Data Generation**

The landscape of machine learning education is currently constrained by a profound scarcity of realistic, causally sound tabular datasets. While the theoretical principles of predictive modeling, causal inference, and feature engineering are rapidly advancing, undergraduate and graduate programs struggle to source data that accurately reflects the noise, confounding, and structural complexities of real-world commercial environments. Proprietary business data is heavily guarded by regulatory frameworks such as the General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR) and the California Consumer Privacy Act (CCPA), rendering it inaccessible for public educational use.1 Conversely, existing public datasets featured on platforms like Kaggle are often over-explored, statically homogeneous, and scrubbed of the complex latent dynamics that characterize genuine business operations.3

To bridge this pedagogical gap, there is a critical need for an advanced programmatic framework capable of generating synthetic datasets specifically tailored to commercial applications, such as lead scoring and Customer Lifetime Value (LTV) prediction. However, traditional synthetic data generation (SDG) techniques are fundamentally ill-suited for this specific educational mandate. Conventional SDG libraries operating within the Python ecosystem, such as the Synthetic Data Vault (SDV), Gretel, and Synthcity, rely heavily on deep learning architectures like Tabular Variational Autoencoders (TVAE), Generative Adversarial Networks (CTGAN), or Gaussian Copulas.5 These paradigms are optimized for privacy-preserving data cloning; they ingest an existing dataset and learn its joint probability distribution, producing synthetic records that mimic the marginal distributions and pair-wise correlations of the original data.9

Because these models are purely associative, they capture correlations without any comprehension of the underlying causal mechanics. An associative generator might replicate a correlation between high regional income and software purchasing, but it remains entirely ignorant of whether income caused the purchase, or if the correlation is spurious and driven by an unobserved confounder such as localized marketing spend.12 If students are to learn how to handle confounding, selection bias, and domain shifts, the underlying dataset must possess a true, verifiable causal architecture.14

Furthermore, a pedagogical framework cannot rely on a singular, fixed causal graph. If the data generation process (DGP) is merely a static directed acyclic graph where only the noise parameters or prior distributions change between iterations, students will inevitably reverse-engineer the invariant structure.16 The modeling task would thus devolve into a trivial exercise in parameter estimation. To provide a deep, adaptable, and powerful educational tool, the framework must elevate the generation process. It must dynamically sample from a vast family of sensible causal graphs, ensuring that every generated dataset presents a unique causal universe that represents different, yet logically sound, real-world business dynamics.17

This comprehensive report details the theoretical, mathematical, and architectural foundations necessary to engineer such a Python-based framework. It synthesizes the domain mechanics of B2B SaaS lead scoring and LTV, explores the implementation of Structural Causal Models (SCMs), details advanced methodologies for sampling from the topological space of sensible causal graphs, outlines critical design alternatives, and proposes a highly modular software architecture.

## **Domain Narrative: B2B SaaS Lead Scoring and Customer Lifetime Value**

To ensure that the generalized mathematical framework produces highly concrete datasets, the generation engine must be anchored in a specific, nuanced business narrative. The Business-to-Business Software-as-a-Service (B2B SaaS) industry provides an optimal vertical for this simulation. The B2B SaaS customer journey is characterized by complex, multi-stakeholder purchasing decisions, prolonged sales cycles, subscription-based revenue models, and rich streams of behavioral telemetry.18

### **The Causal Mechanics of Lead Prioritization**

Lead scoring is the systematic methodology of evaluating and ranking prospective customers based on their mathematical likelihood to convert into paying accounts.21 In a simulated B2B SaaS environment, this conversion probability is not an isolated random variable; it is the terminal node of a complex causal chain influenced by demographic fit, firmographic alignment, and behavioral intent.18

The data generation process must simulate variables across distinct tiers of the prospect identity. Firmographic variables represent the macro-level attributes of the target organization, such as annual recurring revenue, employee headcount, industry sector, and the maturity of their existing technology stack.22 Demographic variables capture the micro-level attributes of the individual lead operating within that organization, encompassing job title, seniority level, and departmental affiliation.25 Together, these explicit data points define the theoretical "fit" of the lead, establishing a causal foundation that determines whether the prospect possesses the budget, authority, and need to purchase the software—a framework commonly referred to in the industry as BANT.28

However, modern predictive lead scoring relies most heavily on implicit, behavioral data, which serves as a highly predictive proxy for buying intent.22 The simulation engine must generate a sequence of engagement metrics that causally flow from the lead's firmographic fit and their exposure to marketing interventions. High-intent behaviors, which should strongly cause an increase in conversion probability within the simulation, include requesting a customized product demonstration, repeatedly visiting pricing pages, or engaging directly with sales development representatives.24 Medium-intent behaviors involve attending industry webinars or downloading technical whitepapers, while low-intent behaviors might merely include opening an automated email newsletter or viewing a generic blog post.24

A realistic dataset generation process must also incorporate negative scoring indicators. Long periods of inactivity, the use of personal email domains rather than corporate addresses, or repeated visits to career pages rather than product pages are behaviors that causally decrease the probability of a closed-won deal.25 The framework must simulate a dynamic where firmographics influence the type of marketing campaigns the lead receives, which in turn influences their behavioral engagement, ultimately culminating in a binary target outcome: conversion to a Marketing Qualified Lead (MQL), transition to a Sales Qualified Lead (SQL), or a closed-won customer.26

### **Modeling Customer Lifetime Value Dynamics**

While lead scoring focuses exclusively on the probability of initial acquisition, Customer Lifetime Value (LTV) prediction estimates the total net revenue a commercial entity can expect from a single customer throughout the entire duration of their relationship.33 Within the framework's roadmap, LTV serves as a critical secondary target variable, as it dictates the maximum allowable Customer Acquisition Cost (CAC) and serves as the foundation for long-term corporate profitability analysis.36

In the B2B SaaS context, LTV is governed by recurring subscription mechanics, making it a complex function of the initial contract value, subsequent expansion revenue through upselling and cross-selling, and eventual customer churn.38 The traditional academic approach to LTV modeling relies on the Recency, Frequency, Monetary (RFM) framework, utilizing probabilistic models such as the Beta-Geometric/Negative Binomial Distribution (BG/NBD) to predict purchase incidence and churn.35 The BG/NBD model explicitly assumes that while a customer is active, their purchases follow a Poisson process, and after each purchase, there is a defined probability that the customer permanently churns.40 The Gamma-Gamma model is then typically applied alongside BG/NBD to estimate the monetary value of future transactions.35

However, for a modern machine learning curriculum, standard RFM and BG/NBD distributions are highly insufficient because they assume behavioral stationarity and actively ignore rich covariate data.40 Modern predictive LTV and churn models leverage complex behavioral telemetry and are driven by advanced algorithms like Gradient Boosted Trees (XGBoost, LightGBM) or deep survival analysis models.42 Therefore, a highly realistic synthetic dataset must generate detailed product telemetry features. These include user adoption rates, the velocity of support ticket creation, shifts in software usage patterns, and the transition of users from basic tiers to enterprise features.38

The underlying data generation process must logically link these post-acquisition behaviors to the eventual lifespan of the customer. For instance, the simulation might introduce a latent variable representing "Customer Onboarding Friction." This unobserved variable would causally decrease early platform logins (an observed telemetry behavior), which in turn dramatically increases the probability of early-stage churn (the final outcome).38

## **Mathematical Foundations: Structural Causal Models (SCMs)**

To fulfill the requirement of an adaptable and powerful Data Generation Process, the framework must transition entirely away from traditional associative data synthesis and embrace causal data synthesis. The generation engine must be constructed upon the rigorous mathematical foundations of Structural Causal Models (SCMs), as originally formalized by Judea Pearl.17

Furthermore, anchoring the framework in SCMs allows the generation engine to seamlessly produce interventional and counterfactual datasets.13 By applying do-calculus interventions to the structural equations—such as programmatically forcing a variable to a specific value regardless of its parents, mimicking a randomized controlled trial or a targeted marketing intervention—the framework can generate datasets that answer "what-if" questions, a capability entirely absent in standard synthetic data tools.14

### **The Necessity of Structural Variance**

A critical mandate of the required framework is that it must not rely on a single, fixed DGP graph. If the framework were to hard-code a single DAG representing the B2B SaaS funnel, merely sampling different parameter weights or prior distributions for each generated dataset, the resulting datasets would be structurally identical.16 In an educational setting, students would quickly recognize the invariant topological structure across assignments. The analytical task would devolve from a rigorous exercise in causal discovery, feature selection, and model debiasing into a trivial exercise in parameter estimation.

Therefore, the framework must elevate the generation process to a meta-level of abstraction. Instead of sampling data points from a static graph, the system must first sample the *graph structure itself* from a defined space of all sensible causal graphs, and only subsequently sample the data points from that newly instantiated architecture.17 This dual-stage sampling process ensures that every generated dataset presents a unique causal topology. This mirrors the commercial reality that the exact drivers of lead conversion vary drastically between different companies, diverse product offerings, and shifting market conditions, providing a continuously challenging environment for machine learning students.

## **Dynamic Structural Sampling: Generating Sensible Causal Graphs**

The primary computational and architectural challenge of this framework lies in dynamically generating a unique, concrete DGP graph while defining and restricting the space of "sensible" graphs. Sampling a DAG uniformly at random from the space of all possible DAGs—using standard graph theory algorithms such as Erdos-Renyi models or Barabasi-Albert preferential attachment—will produce mathematically valid acyclic graphs, but these graphs will blatantly violate fundamental business logic.17

For instance, a purely random graph generation process might easily instantiate a directed edge from a churn\_date node to a company\_founded\_date node, which is chronologically impossible. Similarly, it might propose that reading a blog post directly causes a company's annual revenue to increase. The framework requires a sophisticated generative mechanism that produces vast structural variability while strictly adhering to domain-specific hierarchical constraints.50

### **Implementing Probabilistic Graph Grammars**

To achieve this constrained structural variability, the framework should implement the principles of Probabilistic Graph Grammars. Much like formal grammars in natural language processing generate syntactically valid sentences through iterative rules, a graph grammar consists of a set of production rules that iteratively expand non-terminal symbols into complex, topologically valid graph substructures.52

By viewing the generation of a causal graph as a sequence of derivations over an unambiguous grammar, the framework can construct highly intricate DAGs that represent vastly different strategic marketing and sales architectures while maintaining logical coherence.52 The generation process begins with a high-level abstract node, such as a general Marketing\_Funnel. A production rule might dictate that this node can probabilistically expand into either an Inbound\_Content\_Sequence\_Subgraph, a Webinar\_Driven\_Funnel\_Subgraph, or a Cold\_Outbound\_Sales\_Subgraph.

A probabilistic weighting applied over these production rules allows the engine to sample highly diverse architectural realities. This approach explicitly ensures that long-range dependencies and hierarchical domain constraints are respected.50 If the grammar is strictly defined such that demographic identity nodes can only serve as parents to behavioral nodes, and behavioral nodes can only serve as parents to conversion outcomes, the resulting graph will never contain a reverse-causal loop. This satisfies the requirement that all dynamically generated graphs reflect sensible, realistic views of the underlying commercial dynamic.13

### **Constraint-Based Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) Sampling**

An alternative or complementary approach to utilizing graph grammars is the deployment of Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) methods—specifically Gibbs sampling—over the topological space of DAGs.55 In the field of causal structure learning, algorithms frequently traverse the space of DAGs to find the network structure that best fits an empirical dataset. The proposed framework can ingeniously reverse this paradigm: by defining a target probability distribution over specific graph features, an MCMC sampler can wander through the theoretical graph space and draw a random, perfectly valid DAG.56

To implement this effectively, the domain expert must configure a strict topological tiering constraint system. All possible variables are assigned to distinct, chronological layers:

First, Exogenous Context variables form the root, encompassing geographic regions, industry sectors, and macroeconomic conditions.

Second, Entity Attribute variables are established, including company size, funding stage, and lead seniority.

Third, Latent Driver variables are positioned, representing unobservable factors such as internal budget availability, organizational pain points, and brand awareness.

Fourth, Intervention variables represent corporate actions, such as advertising spend, sales outreach velocity, and customized discount offers.

Fifth, Behavioral variables capture the customer's response, tracking page views, content downloads, and email replies.

Finally, Outcome variables form the terminal nodes, tracking whether the lead score threshold is reached, SQL conversion occurs, and the eventual LTV is realized.

The MCMC sampler is architecturally restricted such that directed edges may only point from lower-numbered tiers to higher-numbered tiers, or within the same tier provided no local cycles are formed.51 This tier-based restriction drastically prunes the infinite search space of all possible DAGs, mathematically ensuring that the algorithm only samples from the specific family of chronologically and logically sound causal structures.51 The sampler can be further tuned to favor sparsity or penalize overly dense hubs, mirroring the structural properties of real-world dependency networks.59

### **Strategic Injection of Latent Confounders and Causal Motifs**

To elevate the pedagogical value of the generated datasets and reward students who correctly utilize advanced causal inference techniques, the graph generation engine should explicitly inject complex causal motifs that mimic the most common data science pitfalls.54

The engine must possess the capability to probabilistically inject unobserved latent confounders into the graph structure. A latent confounder is an exogenous variable that causally influences both an intervention (the treatment) and an outcome, creating a spurious correlation that will bias predictive models if omitted from the analysis.12 For example, the framework might generate a latent node representing Company\_Urgency. This unobserved variable causally drives the lead to both download a technical whitepaper and independently decide to purchase the software. If a student builds a naive predictive model utilizing only observed variables, the model will spuriously over-attribute the conversion outcome directly to the whitepaper download.

By dynamically injecting these specific motifs into the sampled DAG, the framework ensures that the generated datasets serve as exceptionally rigorous testing grounds for causal machine learning curricula.58

## **Open Questions and Design Alternatives**

Engineering a framework of this magnitude requires navigating several highly complex architectural and mathematical trade-offs. The following open questions highlight the primary design alternatives that must be resolved prior to the final software implementation.

### **Functional Assignment: Linear Equations vs. Non-Linear Mechanisms**

Once the graph structure is successfully sampled, the framework must assign the specific structural equations for each node to execute the data generation. The simplest alternative relies on Generalized Linear Models (GLMs). Continuous variables are defined strictly as linear combinations of their causal parents plus Gaussian noise, and discrete variables are generated via standard logistic functions.61 This methodology is highly computationally efficient and allows educators to easily calculate and verify the analytical ground truth of the causal effects. However, real-world CRM data is rarely purely linear, rendering datasets generated this way somewhat artificial. A more advanced alternative involves Neural Network Mechanisms. To simulate true commercial complexity, the framework could assign randomly initialized multi-layer perceptrons (MLPs) or decision tree ensembles to serve as the structural functions, pushing parent data through highly complex non-linear transformations.17 While this generates incredibly complex and realistic distributions, it renders the exact magnitude of the causal effects mathematically opaque, potentially obscuring the ground truth from both the educator and the student. A hybrid, configurable mechanism assigner represents the optimal design choice. By default, the framework should utilize additive noise models with a controlled mix of linear coefficients and bounded non-linear interactions—such as polynomial features or sigmoid transformations. This balances the realism of the dataset with the mathematical tractability required for educational evaluation.17

### **Temporal Dynamics: Static DAGs vs. Dynamic Event Streams**

Lead scoring and LTV prediction are inherently time-dependent processes, with behavioral events occurring sequentially over days, months, or years. The framework could rely exclusively on Static Cross-Sectional DAGs. This approach collapses the commercial timeline into aggregated summary features, such as the total number of website visits in the last thirty days or the days elapsed since the last login. This reduces the complex temporal causal structure to a standard DAG, simplifying the generation process and making the resulting dataset immediately compatible with standard tabular machine learning models like Random Forests.64 Alternatively, the framework could simulate true Dynamic Causal Graphs. This would produce a longitudinal event stream, generating a chronological log of individual touchpoints.66 While this accurately reflects modern enterprise CRM data and allows for the application of recurrent neural networks (RNNs) and time-varying confounder analysis, the generation logic is exponentially more complex. The architectural foundation should initially be built upon static cross-sectional DAGs to satisfy the immediate pedagogical goal of providing tabular lead scoring and LTV datasets.10 However, the core data structures should be engineered with sufficient abstraction to support eventual expansion into Dynamic Bayesian Networks (DBNs), enabling the future simulation of timestamped event logs.68

### **Software Ecosystem: Custom Engineering vs. PPL Integration**

The framework requires a highly robust probabilistic backend to manage the mathematical sampling and the execution of multivariate distributions. Integrating the framework with existing Probabilistic Programming Languages (PPLs) presents a compelling option. Libraries such as PyMC, Pyro, or the causal framework DoWhy provide extensive built-in primitives for defining Graphical Causal Models, automatically executing do-calculus interventions, and seamlessly generating synthetic data from those complex models.48 Leveraging a tool like ChiRho or PyMC allows the framework to offload the heavy mathematical lifting of sampling to highly optimized backends.14 Conversely, relying entirely on heavy PPLs can introduce complex dependencies, potential performance bottlenecks, and steep learning curves for open-source contributors. Developing a custom Graph and Simulation Engine built entirely upon standard, lightweight scientific libraries like NumPy, NetworkX, and SciPy would offer maximal architectural control. This is particularly relevant for managing the unique graph grammar generation process and the highly specific execution of the forward topological data simulation.45 The optimal path forward involves utilizing NetworkX for all internal graph manipulation, DAG sampling, and grammar expansions.45 Once a concrete DAG is generated and finalized by the custom engine, the framework should translate the graph into a standardized format compatible with an established library like PyCausalSim or DoWhy's gcm module to execute the functional data generation and handle counterfactual logic.48

## **Suggested Architectural Design: The CausalCRMGen Framework**

Based on the extensive synthesis of structural causal modeling mathematics, probabilistic graph grammars, and domain-specific B2B SaaS constraints, the proposed Python package should be architected as a highly modular, multi-stage generation engine. The framework—tentatively conceptualized as CausalCRMGen—will decompose the complex synthetic data generation process into distinct, user-controllable layers.4

### **1\. The Domain Configuration Layer (DSL)**

To ensure the generated datasets remain highly concrete and deeply tied to a specific industry narrative, the user primarily interfaces with the framework through a Domain-Specific Language (DSL) or a highly structured configuration object.67 The user defines the specific vertical (e.g., Enterprise Cybersecurity SaaS), the primary target variable (e.g., SQL\_Conversion), and the fundamental boundary constraints.

This configuration file specifies the variable vocabularies, mapping abstract concepts to concrete strings, such as defining company size in realistic revenue tiers rather than continuous floats. Most importantly, it dictates the topological tiering logic that actively prevents cyclical or chronologically impossible structures from forming during the subsequent graph sampling phase, ensuring the domain narrative remains intact.51

### **2\. The Structure Sampling Engine**

This core module is responsible for the dynamic generation of the unique DGP graph. Driven entirely by the rules established in the Domain Configuration Layer, it utilizes the aforementioned Probabilistic Graph Grammar and constrained randomized edge generation algorithms.52

The engine first initializes the root nodes, representing the exogenous context and baseline firmographics. It then expands hidden subgraphs based on probabilistic branching rules. For example, there may be a defined probability that the simulated company relies heavily on outbound cold-calling rather than inbound marketing, resulting in a fundamentally distinct causal structure for the lead outreach portion of the graph. The engine deliberately and probabilistically injects latent confounders and collider structures into the graph, guaranteeing that the final dataset will require students to apply robust causal inference techniques rather than simple correlation mining.58 The output of this stage is a fully formed, topologically sorted Directed Acyclic Graph, maintained internally via NetworkX.45

### **3\. The Functional Mechanism Assigner**

Once the abstract skeleton of the causal graph is firmly established, the framework must breathe mathematical life into it. The Mechanism Assigner module iterates through the topologically sorted nodes and programmatically assigns a specific data-generating function and a noise distribution to every single variable.17

Root nodes are assigned prior empirical distributions, such as an exponential distribution for company revenue or a categorical distribution for the industry sector. Child nodes are assigned structural equations based entirely on the values of their newly established parents. The engine assigns weights, interaction terms, and non-linear boundaries. If the target is a binary classification, such as Lead Conversion, the terminal node is assigned a logistic regression mechanism with a specifically defined threshold.61 Crucially, the exact parameters of these functional equations are sampled from higher-level hyper-parameters, ensuring immense variability even if two generated graphs share similar topologies.

### **4\. The Simulation and Output Engine**

The final architectural stage executes the actual data simulation. Given the fully parameterized SCM, the engine sequentially samples observations.17 For each simulated lead or customer, it draws from the exogenous noise distributions and propagates the data forward through the network according to the assigned structural equations. This executes the entire causal chain from firmographic identity, through behavioral engagement, to the final conversion or LTV outcome.17

The engine then intentionally drops the unobserved latent confounder columns from the final tabular output, perfectly mirroring real-world conditions where the data scientist cannot observe the hidden variables driving the noise in their CRM system.58

The development of robust machine learning curricula requires datasets that transcend the severe limitations of both pristine toy examples and purely associative synthetic data. By architecting a Python framework centered exclusively on Dynamic Structural Causal Models, educators can generate an infinite array of highly concrete, domain-specific datasets tailored to the complex realities of B2B SaaS lead scoring and customer lifetime value prediction. The transition from static data cloning to dynamic structural sampling ensures that every generated dataset possesses a unique underlying data-generating process, forcing students to engage deeply with confounding variables, selection bias, and the challenge of isolating true causal drivers from spurious correlations.
